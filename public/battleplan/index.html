The Talk

WHO → WHY → WHAT I SAW → WHAT WE DID → WHAT YOU CAN DO → TOOLS
Your story leads into the fear. The fear leads into your why. Your why earns you the right to teach them.

Demo 1: Lesson Plan Partner (3 min)

I teach 6th grade ELA. I'm starting a unit on persuasive writing next week. Before you give me a lesson plan, ask me 5 questions about my students so you can personalize it.
AI will ask you questions. Here are your answers — copy and paste each one as it asks:
Q: What subject and grade level? A: 6th grade English Language Arts. Q: What topic or standard? A: Persuasive writing — building an argument with evidence and a clear thesis. Q: How long is your class period? A: 45 minutes. Q: What do students already know? A: They can write a basic 5-paragraph essay, but they struggle with supporting their opinion with real evidence instead of just saying "because I think so." Q: What challenge do they typically face? A: They tend to state their opinion but don't back it up. They also struggle with acknowledging the other side of an argument. Q: Any specific requirements? A: It needs to align with Common Core W.6.1. I have a mix of reading levels, including 3 ELL students. No technology available for students during this lesson — just paper and pencil.
SAY: "Notice what happened. I didn't ask for a lesson plan. I asked it to ask ME questions first. It knows my class size, my ELL students, my time constraints. Now it can build something personalized. That's the partner mindset. Your expertise leads. AI supports."

Demo 2: Differentiation in 60 Seconds (3 min)

Use the lesson plan AI just generated. Paste these follow-ups:
Now adapt this for a student who understands the concepts but struggles with English as a second language. Keep the thinking demands high but simplify the language.
Let it generate. Then immediately paste:
Now create a version for a student who finishes early and needs extension. Challenge them to write a counter-argument to their own thesis.
SAY: "60 seconds. Two differentiated versions. You made every decision. How many of your students know more than they can show you because of a language barrier? AI just removed that barrier. Not the rigor — the barrier."

Demo 3: Student Simulator (2 min)

Act as a 7th grader who is confused about fractions. I'm going to try to explain it to you, and you tell me where my explanation is unclear or where a real student might get lost.
AI will act as a confused student. Give this explanation:
Okay so a fraction is basically a part of a whole. Like if you have a pizza and you cut it into 4 slices and you eat 1 slice, you ate 1/4 of the pizza. The top number is called the numerator and that's how many parts you have. The bottom number is the denominator and that's how many total parts there are. So 3/4 means you have 3 out of 4 parts.
AI will respond as a confused student. Let it play out for 1-2 exchanges. Then say:
SAY: "Look at what just happened. AI found the exact spot where a student would get confused — before you even walked into the classroom. Practice your lesson before class, not during it. Find the gaps before your students do."

Demo 4: Feedback Writer (2 min)

I'm a 5th grade teacher. A student wrote this paragraph about their favorite animal for a descriptive writing assignment: "Dogs are the best pets because they are fun and nice. They play with you and love you. I want a golden retriever because they are fluffy and friendly. Dogs are better than cats because cats are mean." Give feedback the way a great teacher would — specific, encouraging, and focused on one thing to improve. Don't rewrite it for them.
SAY: "Specific. Encouraging. One clear next step. And you decide if it sounds like YOU before it ever reaches the student. That's the partner mindset — AI drafts, you decide."
Part 7

The Toolkit

2 min
Slide 18 — Lesson 1: What AI Is and What It Isn't (switch back to slides)
Switch back from ChatGPT to the presentation. Click to slide 18.
"Those demos are for you — your workflow, your planning. But what about your students? Here's Lesson 1 from our Danbury Library pilot. This is the exact lesson we ran. It builds the foundation before students ever touch AI."
"By the end of this lesson, students believe one thing: 'AI is a tool, not a brain — and my thinking matters.' That's the foundation everything else builds on."
Walk through the 6 steps on screen. Point to each card as you go.
"Step 1 — Set the culture. Before we taught anything, we set three norms: there are no wrong answers, we think first before we use AI, and we respect everyone's ideas. Kids won't think deeply if they don't feel safe. This took two minutes and changed the whole energy."
"Step 2 — Icebreaker. We went around the room. Name, favorite color, dogs or cats — and you have to say WHY. That 'why' matters. We're already practicing explaining your reasoning. Talking IS thinking."
"Step 3 — Mind map. Hand out blank paper. 'Draw everything you think you know about AI.' What it is, what it can do, where you've seen it. No talking. No phones. Just your brain on paper. This becomes your baseline — your before picture."
"Step 4 — Turn and talk. Students pair up. 'Teach your partner one thing you know about AI.' Now they're engaging, they're sharing, and you're walking around observing — not correcting. You're seeing what they actually believe."
"Step 5 — The 4 truths. This is conversational, not a lecture. Ask: 'Who wants to share something from their mind map?' Then you push them toward four core ideas. AI does not think. It predicts the next word. AI does not understand. It recognizes patterns. AI does not know truth. It can sound confident and be completely wrong. AI is made by humans. Humans decide what matters. This becomes their mental anchor for everything that comes after."
"Step 6 — Reflect. Students look back at their mind map. 'Would you add anything? Cross anything out? What surprised you?' That's metacognition. That's the moment they realize their understanding just changed — and they can see it on paper."
"This lesson takes about 45 minutes when you run it fully. You can compress it to 15-20 minutes for a quick intro. The point is: before students ever open ChatGPT, they know what AI actually is. That foundation prevents every problem you're worried about — cheating, over-reliance, outsourcing thinking. Because they understand the tool before they use it."
